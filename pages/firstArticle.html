The Basis of All Diets: Energy Conservation

First, the most import law that must be understood as the general basis for all nutrition related topics is the Law of Thermodynamics (specifically the 1st law). It is a fundamental law that governs the physical characteristics of the universe. The first Law of Thermodynamics states that energy is conserved, it cannot be created nor can it be destroyed, it can only be transformed from one form to another (Walsh 413)(Hall 989). So what does that mean? Calories is a unit of energy, specifically 1 kilocalorie equates to 4184 Joules of energy. For the rest of this article I’m going to refer 1 kilocalorie to 1 Calorie, notice the capitalization of the letter c, which is often used to denote 1 kilocalorie and is different from calorie. 1000 calorie equals to 1 Calorie or 1 kilocalorie (Walsh 413). That may have sound confusing but this is how it is defined, unfortunately. But you don’t have to worry so much about that, just know Calories in the food label is synonymous to kilocalories. Since Calories equates to 4184 Joules of energy, calories is essentially energy itself so it still holds up to the law of Thermodynamics. So calories cannot just disappear, it can only be moved to a different location or be stored or expended (keep that in your thought).

Calories can be calculated because it is on all nutrition labels of food, so the great part is that you’ll know how much calories you are consuming if you add up the total amount of calories from the foods you ate. Calories in and Calories out (CICO) is the idea that weight gain or weight loss is determined by caloric surplus or caloric deficit, regardless of diet composition (Aragon et al). If the amount of calories you consume is less than the amount of calories you burn (you burn calories through TDEE, more on that later) you will be in a caloric deficit, meaning you’re going to be burning fat (and even lean body mass to an extent depending on multiple factors, more on that in a later articles). If however the amount of calories you are consuming is greater than the amount of calories you are burning, you will be in a caloric surplus and this can result in fat gain and also muscle gain if you do your resistance training (you do not have to be in a caloric surplus to gain muscle but more on that in a later article).

So as I mentioned earlier, what is TDEE? TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure and it accounts for 3 main variables. TDEE is the sum of the resting metabolic rate (RMR), the thermic effect of food (TEF) and the energy expenditure in physical activity (EEPA) (McManus, 368)(Levine 667)(DeLany 85-102)(Levine & Kotz, 309). EEPA can be broken down into two subcomponents, non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) and exercise activity thermogenesis (EAT) (Levine 667).

RMR or resting metabolic rate is the minimal amount of calories required just to stay alive (Schrack, 667). There are many factors that influences RMR such as genetics, age, gender, fat mass, fat-free mass (FFM), aerobic fitness level, environmental temperature and altitude, hormonal factors, stress, etc. (Broeder 795). RMR accounts for the largest single component of TDEE (Itoi et al. 64), up to 50%-70% of total caloric expenditure for most people (McManus, 368)(Aragon et al)(Levine & Kotz, 309). The reason why your body requires so much energy just to keep you alive is because there are many, many things going on in the body that all requires energy. It does not require so much energy individually, but in totality it requires an extraordinary amount. These such things includes cellular functioning, contraction of the muscles of the heart, contraction of the smooth muscles that make up the organs in your body, releases of hormones, enzyme activities, digestion, neurons of the brain firing, etc. All these occur every seconds (some even milliseconds) of every minute of every hour of every day till the day you die. That is a ton of energy being expended just to keep you alive, which is why again that RMR takes up majority of the calories expended of TDEE.

TEF or thermic effect of food accounts for only a small percentage of TDEE, generally about 10% (Binns 204)(McManus, 368). This percentage can vary but not significantly. TEF is the energy expenditure from the digestion, absorption, processing and disposal of digested food (Hall 990)(Crovetti 482)(Levine 667). So to simplify its meaning, eating food burns calories but you will only burn a small portion of the calories of the food you consume (some food will lead to greater expenditure of calories in ratio to its calorie content compared to others). Protein has a higher TEF compared to carbs and fats and it is also more satiating (Crovetti 482)(Binns 204). So a good fat loss strategy is to increase your protein intake.

EAT or exercise activity thermogenesis accounts for a portion of TDEE and it can vary greatly from person to person. It is the calories burned through exercise (Aragon et al). And not everyone exercises in a regular basis or even at all, but that is one end of the spectrum whereas some athletes will exercise twice a day almost everyday. These athlete will burn a lot more calories throughout the day than the sedentary individual. And that is why there is such a great discrepancy in calories burned through TEE amongst many people.

NEAT or non-exercise activity thermogenesis takes up a chunk of TDEE and it can also vary greatly between person to person, even more so than EAT. NEAT is the energy expenditure of everything we do except for sleeping, eating and exercise. So things like blinking, working, talking, hand gestures, walking, fidgeting, etc (Levine 667)(Levine & Kotz, 309)(Aragon et al) are all subcategorized under NEAT. The reason it can vary so much between individuals is because of those thing such as work. There are some people who are very active in their jobs such as construction workers or bussers in busy restaurants and others who have sedentary jobs such as desk jobs. Between the two there is great discrepancy in physical activity. The other factors can also play a huge role in differences in calories burned through NEAT such as hand gestures, fidgeting and talking. Throughout the day the more an individual is active in those factors, so someone who is very social vs someone who is not can result in big differences in how much calories is burned throughout the day. But that is just an example, there are multiple other scenarios and factors in which why 1 person may burn a lot more calories through NEAT compared to another.

As mentioned earlier, NEAT and EAT can both be subcategorized into EEPA and it is also represented in the graph on TDEE as activity energy expenditure. Of the big three, EEPA, TEF, and RMR, EEPA is the most flexible in that it can vary greatly between individuals (Levine 667)(Levine & Kotz, 309). Even it’s individual components which I’ve just mentioned TEA and NEAT varies greatly. EEPA can take as little as 15% of TDEE or up to 50% of TDEE (McManus, 368).

In conclusion, the 1st Law of Thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created nor can it be destroyed, it can only be transferred from one location to another. This concept is related to calories (which is a unit of energy so it abides by this universal law) and it explains why calories is the fundamental and basis for all diets alike. If one is is in a caloric deficit, one will lose weight. Likewise, if one is in a caloric surplus, one will gain weight. And in between the two if calories burned equals to calories consumed, one will be at maintenance. Your next question may be, why is it that my weight changes day to day even though I’m at caloric maintenance? That question will be answered in a another article as it requires greater details. What accounts for the total amount of calories we burn each day is equated into TDEE. And it can be broken down into 3 main categories, resting metabolic rate (RMR), thermic effect of food (TEF), energy expenditure of physical activity (EEPA).

Understanding these fundamentals will give you a basis to knowing how all diet works. The next step is doing some calculations to find out whether you’re in a caloric surplus, deficit, or maintenance. The first step to that is finding the total amount of calories you burn throughout the day. You can do this simply by looking up “calculating TDEE” on google and you’ll be able to find different websites that provides calculation on how to find how much calories you expend in a day. (However, remember these are all just estimations, they are not 100% accurate of your maintenance calories but it will give you a reasonable estimate of it even if it is within a few hundred calories of deviation). You may get different estimations from different sites because they may use different calculations and the next best thing to do is to stick to that estimation and track your weight on a daily basis and then average it out for the week. Then compare the weekly average to depict an accurate reading of whether you are gaining, losing or maintaining weight. Below is a way on how to make that calculation.

Remember that of all the components of TDEE, EAA can vary the most between individuals, so be very honest with your estimation and if you feel like you’re in range between 2 categories, average those out and then multiply it with your RMR.

For example if you’re in range between light exercise and moderate exercise, meaning you work out 2x week but because you are pretty active at work you may feel like you’re at moderate exercise level or slightly below, you can choose a number in between light exercise and moderate exercise to multiply it with your calculated BMR. So you do not have to be committed to the numbers on that chart, assume which exercise level or levels that you’re in and put in what daily calorie needs best fits your description of your lifestyle.

Now that you found the amount of calories you expend each day, you now want to track how many calories you consume each day. You can do this by using myFitnessPal or any other app that tracks the calories of food. Be honest with yourself so don’t underreport or overreport the calories of the foods that you consume. Also use measuring utensils such as measuring cups, measuring spoons, and a food scale to give you a more accurate reading of how many calories that particular food has. You’ll be surprise in how much or how little serving size for certain foods are. So start using measuring utensils to give you a progressively better estimate and gauge of serving size of the regular foods you consume.

Subtract your calculated energy expenditure with the total calories of the food you consume for the day and you should have a net balance. For example: let’s say you burn 3000 Calories per day and you consume approximately 2500 Calories per day. Your net balance is 3000 - 2500 = -500 calories. You will be at a caloric deficit as it is below 0. So you will be losing weight if you keep this net negative balance rate over time. This will approximate to losing up to 1lb of weight per week. And congrats you just learned how to lose weight with a simple math problem. If you want to gain weight then in this scenario you will be consuming over 3000 calories so you can be in a positive net balance, meaning you will be in a caloric surplus.

Now you should be all set. You now have the understanding of the fundamentals of nutrition. With the knowledge of how energy works and the knowledge about the components that comprises of TDEE you will not fall for any gimmicks or new trendy diets. You also now know how to estimate how many calories you are burning and with tracking your own calories and knowing the best way to track weight, you can stay on track of your progress and determine if you are getting closer and closer to reaching your goal of weight loss or weight gain or weight maintenance.
