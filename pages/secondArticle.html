The Science Behind Heart Rate Variability and It's Role with the Autonomic Nervous System

For the past four decades heart rate variability (HRV) has increasingly been used as a biomarker for the assessment of autonomic functions and cardiac health (Vescio, Salsone, Gambardella, & Quattrone, 2018). But what exactly is HRV, how can it be measured and what’s it role with the autonomic nervous system (ANS)? Well, HRV is measured from the use of measuring heart rate (HR). HR is the number of heartbeats per unit of time, which is usually one minute. So HRV can be measured from the differences in intervals of time between each successful heartbeats. And that gets into what exactly HRV is. By looking at the differences in intervals of time between each successful heartbeats, you may see variability in the time it takes from one heartbeat to occur to another, and to another, and to another, and so on. This variability in time between each heartbeats is what HRV is.

To answer the second part of the question, HRV can be measured or is usually measured by observing an electrocardiographic signal, which is detected from a monitoring system called an electrocardiogram (ECG). When using an ECG, one can measure the time interval of heartbeats by looking at the peak of the QRS waves. The peak of the QRS wave is represented by the letter R. The R-R interval or its synonymous term, interbeat intervals (IBI) are the measurement of the time intervals between any two adjacent R peaks in an ECG wave (Vescio, Salsone, Gambardella, & Quattrone, 2018).

And to answer the third part of the question I’ll be going go into the physiology of the ANS like what exactly it is and how it affects us because it is of most importance to really understanding its function, which will make it easier to understand its role with HRV. Based on the studies I’ve come across, HRV has a lot to do with the autonomic nervous system because it is a huge influencer on heart rate and its variability.

Looking into the broad aspect of the nervous system, it has two main branches, one of which is the peripheral nervous system (PNS), which is composed of two major divisions, the motor division and the sensory division. Of these two major divisions of the PNS that we should take a more particular interest in, is the motor division, which includes the ANS. The ANS is often considered part of the motor division and it controls the body’s involuntary internal functions (Kenney, Wilmore, & Costill, p. 85). The sensory division also plays a role in affecting HRV through mechanisms such as the effects from baroreceptors, chemoreceptors and circulating hormones. The ANS is comprised of the parasympathetic nervous system and the sympathetic nervous system (Dong, 2016). They play an important role in physical activity, exercise, and even at rest by affecting heart rate, blood pressure, and lung function. They regulate processes that are important for maintaining physiological homeostasis and responding to acute stressors. They function independently of other adaptive systems (Kenney & Ganta, 2014). They also have opposing affects but always work together. They originate from different sections of the spinal cord (Kenney, Wilmore, & Costill, p. 85).

The sympathetic nervous system is sometimes referred to as the fight-or-flight system because of its multitude of physiological responses that prepares the body to face a crisis. These multitude of physiological responses of the sympathetic nervous system includes: increasing heart rate as well as the force of contraction, vasodilation of blood vessels, bronchodilation in the lungs, increase blood pressure, and stimulating the release of the catecholamines, epinephrine and norepinephrine. There are other physiological responses from the sympathetic nervous system, but these physiological responses are of more concern to HRV. The parasympathetic nervous system has opposing effects with the exception to blood pressure where it has little to no effect on it and also for the catecholamines where it has no effect on it as well. The parasympathetic nervous system is much more active at rest and helps with digestion, urination, and conservation of energy. The parasympathetic nerve is often referred to as the vagal nerve because the parasympathetic supply to the heart runs in the vagal nerves (Aubert, Seps, & Beckers, 2003).

That summarizes what the ANS is, what it is composed of, and how it effects us. Now I’m going to connect it to HRV.

In the first few paragraphs I described what HRV is and how it can be measured, but I have yet to talk about why there is variability in time intervals between heartbeats or to put more simply and shortly, the oscillation in the heart rate. The oscillation in the heart rate represents a healthy heart. And a healthy heart is complex and constantly changing, which allows the cardiovascular system to rapidly adjust to sudden physical and psychological challenges to homeostasis (Shaffer & Ginsberg, 2017).

The mechanism behind the oscillation in the heart rate can be due to the integration between the nervous system and the cardiovascular system. But more specifically between the inputs of the cardiovascular regulatory centers located in the spinal cord and medulla and the input of the higher brain center. The integration between those higher functioning mechanisms helps adjust heart rate and blood pressure via sympathetic and parasympathetic efferent pathways (McCraty & Shaffer, 2015). The medulla is the primary site to regulate the parasympathetic and sympathetic nerves outflow to the heart and blood vessels. To go into further details, the nucleus tractus solitarius located in the medulla receives sensory input and stimulates cardiovascular responses to emotion and stress (Makivic, Nikic, & Willis, 2013).

Because there is integration between the heart and the efferent pathways of the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous system, HR is regulated by these systems. There is a balance between these two systems, which affects the oscillation in heartbeats, which again defines what HRV is (Makivic, Nikic, & Willis, 2013). But exactly how do they affect HRV? Well, they innervate the heart and its conduction system.

The ANS supplies both the afferent and efferent nerves to the heart, with the bulk of the myocardium of the heart targeted by the sympathetic nerves and the vagal nerves on the SA node (Aubert, Seps, & Beckers, 2003). Earlier I mentioned about one of the physiological functions of the sympathetic nervous system in which they release epinephrine and norepinephrine. When action potential are conducted by the sympathetic nerves, it releases these catecholamines, which leads to an increase in HR and an increase in the heart’s force of contraction. But there is a delay in the onset of sympathetic activation on the heart, which for a brief moment is five seconds. It can be much longer than that for ongoing sympathetic activation. What this means is that the sympathetic nervous system takes too long to take an affect on HRV because HRV varies by milliseconds. In contrast the vagal stimulation is much more quicker, practically almost instantaneous. So primary changes in the oscillation of the HR is mostly mediated by the parasympathetic nervous system (McCraty & Shafer,2015).

In contrast to what was mentioned on the paper by McCraty & Shafer, “Heart Rate Variability: New Perspectives on Physiological Mechanisms, Assessment of Self-Regulatory Capacity, and Health Risk,” the sympathetic nervous system does have some indirect influence on HRV by affecting HR.

In a case when there is no ANS activity on the heart, the intrinsic rate generated by the SA node is about 100 beats per minute (bpm). The vagal nerves are the predominant factor influencing HR during regular physical activity, rest, or sleep when HR is below a 100 bpm (White & Raven, 2014). So essentially you can think of the vagal nerves as the factor that slows down HR to below 100 bpm. During mild or intense exertions of physical activity or exercise, as the HR exceeds 100 bpm, the sympathetic nerves becomes the predominant influencer (McCraty & Shafer, 2015). But as HR increases from resting levels to greater than 100 bpm due to mild or intense exertions of physical activity or exercise, this increase in HR is the result of the withdrawal of the vagal nerves (White & Raven, 2014).

As I mentioned previously the sympathetic nervous system has more of a role on the effect on just the HR. But because of that it has an indirect influence in HRV. When HR increases, there is a reduce time between successive heartbeats and the opportunity for the R-R interval to vary decreases. This lowers HRV, whereas slower HR increases the time between adjacent heartbeats and the chance for R-R interval to vary, which increases HRV (Shaffer & Ginsberg, 2017). The relative sympathetic nerve increases causes the R-R intervals to become shorter (Thayer, Fredrikson, Sollers III, & Wager, 2011). So as HRV decreases, HR increases, which shows an inverse relationship between the two. And the sympathetic nervous system can be attributed to this effect.

As mentioned earlier, oscillation in heartbeats or HRV represents a healthy heart because it allows the cardiovascular system to rapidly adjust to sudden physical and psychological challenges to homeostasis (Shaffer & Ginsberg, 2017). But over time the oscillation of the heartbeat or HRV will decrease and not just acutely such as from exercise, which is a normal healthy physiological function, but progressively with aging. Reduced HRV also correlates with and predicts mortality rates in populations with high cardiovascular disease (CVD) and low CVD (Borchini et al., 2018).

According to a paper by Younes and colleagues, HRV is strongly influenced by age, its amplitude increases between childhood and adolescence but in adults it starts decreasing with age (Younes, Robert, Barrey, & Cottin, 2016). Though the paper was based on a study on male endurance horses, it showed that HRV amplitude decreased with age. HRV was also influenced by other factors such as exercise duration, temperature, humidity, weather, and ground surface (Younes, Robert, Barrey, & Cottin, 2016). But those factors were acute, where as aging is progressive, which has more of chronic impact on HRV. What results in lower HRV from aging is due to a reduction in vagal or parasympathetic activity (Umetani, Duda, & Singer, 1996). In another paper by Monfredi and colleagues, it suggests that the correlation between a decrease in HRV and higher morbidity and mortality is the consequence of the concurrent increase in HR (Monfredi et al., 2014). Which supports the earlier statement because there is generally a concurrent increase in HR as people age into the their elderly stage.

From the previous paper I mentioned about earlier on how exercise duration, temperature, humidity, weather, and ground surface can affect HRV, from a sports performance perspective; that can be very relevant. And using HRV as a parameter to monitor recovery, the ANS is evaluated through different methods. The most commonly used methods are frequency-domain, time-domain, and Poincare plot parameters (Makivic, Nikic, & Willis, 2013).

The frequency-domain analysis describes high and low frequency rates of the variability changes, corresponding to the activity of different branches of ANS. By applying these frequency differences in HRV analysis, the individual contribution of parasympathetic and sympathetic systems were identified (Dong, 2016). The individual contributions for both the parasympathetic and sympathetic systems from HRV analysis are also identified in the other methods through different statistical measurements. Continuing on with the frequency-domain analysis, low frequency (LF) modulation (0.04-0.15 Hz) of R-R interval changes corresponds to the sympathetic and parasympathetic activities acting together. High frequency (HF) modulation (0.15-0.4 Hz) of R-R interval changes is primarily regulated through innervation of the heart through the parasympathetic nerve (Makivic, Nikic, & Willis, 2013).

In the time-domain parameters, the standard deviation (SD) of all the N-N intervals (SDNN) reflects total variability of the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems. And the root mean square of SDs (RMSSD) between adjacent N-N intervals represents parasympathetic activity (Makivic, Nikic, & Willis, 2013). The N-N interval is synonymous with the term R-R interval. In the In the Poincare plot, the standard descriptor 1 (SD1) represents short-term variability in the heart and standard descriptor 2 (SD2) describes long-term variability in the heart (Fishman, Jacono, & Dick, 2012).

These are the three most commonly methods used for ANS analysis used in many research papers to gauge HRV recovery amongst people and animals, which helps look into a sports performance perspective. Changes of ANS activity during and after exercise could provide a great deal of physiological and pathological information (Xiang Chen et al., 2017).

In an example of a paper published by James and colleagues sixteen experienced runners participated for the study. Measurements of blood pressure and HRV were recorded 1 hour prior, 1 hour, 24 hours, 48 hours, and 72 hours post exercise sessions. Subjects were not allowed to perform any exercise 72 hours prior to the first assessment or in between any subsequent assessments. Assessments were done in a laboratory under controlled conditions such as time of day, humidity, atmospheric pressure, and temperature.

The finding of the study suggests that severe exercise reduced indicators of parasympathetic influence on the heart, which was measured from the analysis of the ANS through the Poincare plot and frequency domain analysis. It showed statistical significance on SDNN, RMSSD, & HF values. There was also an indicator of the sympathetic influence 1 hour post exercise bout from LF value. (James, Munson, Maldonado-Martin, & Croix, 2012). The values reversed 24 hours post exercise bout and then remained constant 48 hours and 72 hours post exercise bout. Moderate intensity exercise had no change in either sympathetic or parasympathetic influence on the heart.

In conclusion, HRV is the oscillation in heartbeats, specifically the variability in the interval of time between R-R intervals on a QRS wave, which can be tracked on an ECG. There are other modalities of tracking HRV, but this is the most common. HRV is associated with the integration between the heart and the ANS. The parasympathetic nervous system have much more of an influence on HRV because the stimulation it has on the heart is much more quick and instantaneous compared to the sympathetic nervous system. And by analyzing the ANS activity, researchers, coaches, doctors, etc., are able to monitor recovery/fatigue, determine certain types of health disparities, and determine what environmental factors may influence performance and recovery.
